00

Introduction

Swiss German is a continuum of Alemannic dialects spoken across German-speaking Switzerland. This guide is focused on Zürich German, the dialect of the canton and city of Zürich, specifically the city dialect.

This guide assumes a working knowledge of Standard German (cases, gender, verb conjugation, separable prefixes), and focuses on how Zürich German differs from it.

There is no single standardized spelling for Swiss German; this guide uses the Dieth-Schreibung conventions where practical.[Dieth]

01

Phonology

Sound shifts from Standard German

Most differences between Standard German and Zürich German are predictable from a handful of historical sound correspondences. In several cases, Zürich German simply preserves a Middle High German form that Standard German has since monophthongized or diphthongized.[de.wp]

A monophthong is a single vowel sound (like the ee in see); a diphthong is a vowel sound that glides from one position to another (like the oy in boy or the ow in now). To monophthongize is to collapse a diphthong into a single vowel; to diphthongize is the reverse.

Shift Standard German Zürich German Notes
[aʊ] → [uː] Haus, Ausgang Huus, Uusgang Only where Standard German au comes from Middle High German /uː/. See au → uu.
[aɪ] → [iː] Schweiz, Zeit, Wein Schwiiz, Ziit, Wii Only where SG ei comes from MHG /iː/. Where it comes from MHG /ei/, it stays as äi: Stein → Stäi.
[iː] → [iə] Liebe, Brief Liebi, Briäf Zürich German preserves MHG /ie/, /uo/, /üe/. SG monophthongized them.
[uː] → [uə] Fuß, gut, Bruder Fuess, guet, Brueder Same pattern as above: preserves MHG /uo/.
[ɔɪ] → [yː] Deutsch, Freund, heute Tüütsch, Fründ, hüt SG eu/äu from MHG /iu/ stays as ü(ü).
[k] → [x] / [kx] Kind, Kalt, Kuchen Chind, chalt, Chueche The famous Swiss ch. See below.

Final -n is dropped almost everywhere: machen → mache, kommen → choo. The infinitive ending -en reduces to -e. Inside words and at word-end, st and sp are pronounced (and in Dieth often written) scht / schp, so ist → isch, bist → bisch, Fest → Fescht, Post → Poscht. Word-initially they stay written as st-/sp- but are still pronounced scht/schp.[Verein]

The k → ch / kx shift

High Alemannic completed the second Germanic consonant shift further than Standard German did. Native /k/ became the velar fricative [x] (written ch) word-initially. Loanwords from Standard German that entered later get the affricate [kx] instead.[de.wp]

Native k → ch [x]

  • Kind → Chind
  • kalt → chalt
  • Kuchen → Chueche
  • Küche → Chuchi
  • Kopf → Chopf
  • kommen → choo
  • danken → danche

Loanword k → kx

  • Kafi
  • Kanton
  • Kino
  • Kampf
  • Kanal

These are written with k but pronounced with a noticeably "harder" onset than Standard German.

The au → uu shift in detail

Words where Standard German au comes from MHG /uː/ shift to uu. Words where it comes from MHG /ou/ keep a diphthong (often au in Zürich, ou in other regions).[de.wp]

Shifts to uu

  • Auszeit → Uusziit
  • Aufbau → Uufbuu
  • Bauch → Buuch
  • Faust → Fuust
  • Kraut → Chruut
  • Laus → Luus
  • Maus → Muus
  • Strauch → Struuch
  • Schaufel → Schuufle

Stays a diphthong

  • Auto
  • Auge → Aug
  • Pause
  • Lauch
  • Rauch → Rauch / Rouch
  • Zauberer

Articles

Articles follow the same gender / number / case logic as Standard German, with two important caveats:

  • The genitive case does not exist in spoken Swiss German. Possession is expressed via vo + dative, or by a possessive-dative construction (see below).
  • Several article forms come in long / short variants, the longer form is used before a vowel, the shorter one before a consonant.

Definite article

Nominative de(r)* s d d
Accusative de(n)* s d d
Dative em em de(r)* de(n)*

* The longer forms (in parentheses) are used when a vowel follows, the shorter ones before a consonant. De Maa (the man) vs. der Esel (the donkey).[Nthuleen]

Indefinite article

Masculine Neuter Feminine
Nominative, Accusative en es e(n)*

Short dative forms eme(n) (m/n) also occur; the longer forms are more typical of Zürich.

Negative article

Dative kä(i)m kä(i)m kä(i)nere(n) kä(i)ne

The käi- vs kä- variation is regional: the city tends toward kä-, Winterthur / Oberland toward käi-.

Articles before personal names

In Zürich German, and most Swiss German dialects, personal names almost always take an article. De Hans, d Anna, s Lisi. Omitting the article sounds strangely formal, like reading from a passport. This is one of the easiest tells that someone is speaking Swiss German and not Standard German.

De Hans hät mer s Buech ggëë.
Hans hat mir das Buch gegeben.
Hans gave me the book.

Expressing possession (no genitive)

Because there is no genitive, possession is expressed two ways:

  • vo + dative: s Velo vom Peter (Peter's bike).
  • Possessive-dative construction: em Peter sis Velo (lit. "to-the Peter his bike"). The possessor is in the dative and followed by a possessive pronoun agreeing with the possessed item.
Em Peter sis Velo isch kapuut.
Peters Fahrrad ist kaputt.
Peter's bike is broken.

04

Verbs

The verb system is where Swiss German diverges most from Standard German. The main differences:

  1. All three plural persons share the same form. The 1st, 2nd, and 3rd person plural conjugate identically. Weak verbs take -ed (mir mached, ir mached, si mached); most irregular verbs and modals take -nd (mir gönd, ir gönd, si gönd).
  2. There is no preterite. Past tense is always the Perfekt.
  3. Future tense is usually expressed by the present tense with an adverb (morn, spöter). The wärde-future (wärde + infinitive) exists but is used less freely than in Standard German.
  4. Several auxiliary verbs are "doubled" with a particle go, cho, or la.
  5. The Konjunktiv II with -ti survives and is in everyday use (whereas the preterite that it's built on has disappeared).

Core irregular verbs

The plural -nd ending fuses with a postposed pronoun: gönd + mir = gömer ("we go" / "shall we go"), händ + mir = händer / hämmer ("we have"), sind + mir = simer ("we are").

er / sie / es isch hät gaat tuet wird

participle gsii ghaa ggange taa worde

Sandhi: word-final -n resurfaces before a vowel-initial pronoun or particle, ich bi-n-en gsee ("I saw him"). [tal-mi-or]

No preterite, use the Perfekt

Swiss German has no preterite / simple past tense. Whatever past tense you want to express, you build the Perfekt with haa or sii + past participle. This is true of all spoken Swiss German.

Gestern war ich zu Hause.
Gester bini dihei gsii.

Subjunctive II (Konjunktiv II)

Even though the preterite is gone, the Konjunktiv II built from it survives and is in daily use for hypotheticals, polite requests, and counterfactuals. It's typically formed with the suffix -ti on the verb stem, often clipped to -t in the singular. Würde + infinitive is unusual; the synthetic form is preferred.[Nübling]

Verb Konjunktiv II (1sg) Example
sii (sein) ich wär / wäri Wenn ich riich wär …
haa (haben) ich hät / hätti Ich hätti gärn es Bier.
gah (gehen) ich gieng / göng Ich gieng au gärn mit.
chöne (können) ich chönt / chönti Chöntsch mer hälfe?
wele (wollen) ich wett / wetti Ich wett en Kafi.
müese (müssen) ich müesst / müessti Du müesstisch früener cho.
tue (tun) ich täät / täti Ich täti scho cho, wenn …
choo (kommen) ich chäm / chämti Chämsch morn?

Polite ordering, Ich hätti gärn …, is the equivalent of "I'd like …" in restaurants and shops, and is one of the most useful Konjunktiv II forms to drill.

Doubled verbs (go, cho, la)

A small set of motion / causative verbs, gah, choo, laa, aafaa, spawn a "doubled" particle when chained with another verb. The doubled form is a clitic-like marker that sits in front of the main verb.

Verb Doubled form English
gah (gehen) go to go
choo (kommen) cho to come
laa (lassen) la to let
aafaa (anfangen) aa to begin
Ich gang go poschte.

I'm going (out) shopping.
Chum cho ässe!
Komm essen!
Come eat!
La mich la mache!
Lass mich machen!
Let me do it!

The tue-periphrasis

Alemannic dialects (Swiss German among them) use tue + infinitive much more readily than Standard German. The construction marks durative or progressive aspect (Mir tüend grad es Bild ufhänke, "We're [in the middle of] hanging a picture"), and also serves as a Konjunktiv II auxiliary for verbs that lack their own synthetic subjunctive form.[Erb 2009]

Ich tue grad lese.
I'm reading right now.
Ich täti gärn cho.
I would like to come. (≈ würde gerne kommen)

Verbs that differ from Standard German

Some verbs are not just phonetically shifted but use a different root altogether. A handful of "false friends" to know:

  • sprechen → rede   (Ich rede Änglisch.)
  • gehen (to walk) → laufe
  • laufen (to run) → renne or springe (Swiss springe means both "jump" and "run")
English Standard German Zürich German Example
to call up anrufen alüte Lüt mir a, wenn d acho bisch.
to look schauen luege Lueg deet de Elifant!
to listen (an-, zu-)hören lose Im Zug tueni mäischtens Musig lose.
05

Syntax

Relative clauses

Zürich German has exactly one relative pronoun: wo (or won before a vowel). Whatever case relationship the relative would mark in Standard German is instead carried by a resumptive pronoun elsewhere in the clause.[Salzmann]

Resumptive pronouns are obligatory for dative, oblique, and possessive relations, but not used for subjects or direct objects in standard Zürich usage.

  • wo + ich contracts to woni
  • wo + mer (1pl) contracts to womer

Subject relative, no resumptive

The woman who always comes too late.
Die Frau, die immer zu spät kommt.
D Frau, wo immer z spaat chunt.

Direct object relative, no resumptive

A picture that nobody can pay for.
Ein Bild, das niemand bezahlen kann.
Es Bild, wo niemert cha zale.

Dative relative, resumptive pronoun

The boy to whom we promised a bike.
Der Junge, dem wir ein Fahrrad versprochen haben.
De Bueb, womer em es Velo versproche händ.

Oblique (with preposition), resumptive pronoun

The friend with whom I always go drinking.
Der Freund, mit dem ich immer saufen gehe.
De Fründ, woni immer mit em gang go suufe.

Negation

The default marker of negation in the city dialect is nöd (in Winterthur and the eastern part of the canton, often nid).

Ich wott di nöd gsee.
I don't want to see you.

When the sentence contains an indefinite noun phrase, negation is marked by the negative article in front of that noun phrase rather than by nöd:

Ich wott kä Kafi.
I don't want any coffee.

Diminutive -li

Swiss German is famously fond of the diminutive suffix -li. It corresponds roughly to Standard German -chen / -lein, but is far more productive: almost any noun can take it. The plural is invariant (no extra ending).

Base Diminutive Meaning
Hund Hündli little dog
Chind Chindli little child
Maa Männli little man
Bizli es Bizli / es bitzeli a (little) bit
Kafi Käffeli a (small) coffee
Bier Bierli a (small) beer

Note the typical umlaut: Hund → Hündli, Maa → Männli, Kafi → Käffeli. Many extremely common Swiss nouns are only used in diminutive form: es Müesli, es Bizli, es Briefli.

Particles and flavor words

Discourse particles are short, often untranslatable words that carry tone, register, or attitude. Using them is what makes Swiss German sound like Swiss German rather than Standard German with shifted vowels.

Words that exist in Standard German but mean something different in Swiss German, or that differ enough in form to trip up a learner who knows German.

Sample dialogue

A casual conversation between two friends planning a trip to Zürich. Each turn is given in Zürich German, Standard German, and English.

A Häsch gwüsst, eusi Kollegin d Barbara hüratet nächst Wuche.
Hast du gewusst, unsere Kollegin Barbara heiratet nächste Woche.
You know, our colleague Barbara is getting married next week.
B Ja, sie hät mich au iglade. Bisch schomal z Züri gsii?
Ja, sie hat mich auch eingeladen. Warst du schon mal in Zürich?
Yes, she invited me too. Have you ever been to Zürich?
A Nei, ich bin nonie z Züri gsii.
Nein, ich war noch nie in Zürich.
No, I've never been to Zürich.
B Wie wär's, wenn mer zäme deet ane fahred?
Wie wäre es, wenn wir gemeinsam dorthin fahren?
How about we go there together?
A Das isch e sehr gueti Idee.
Das ist eine sehr gute Idee.
That's a very good idea.
B Wie wämmer uf Züri fahre? Mitem Zug?
Wie wollen wir nach Zürich fahren? Mit der Bahn?
How do you want to get to Zürich? By train?
A Nei, mitem Zug gaht's z lang und mir müend viel umstiige.
Nein, mit der Bahn dauert es lange und wir müssen viel umsteigen.
No, by train it takes a long time and we have to change trains a lot.
B Mir chönnted au mitem Auto fahre. Was meinsch?
Wir könnten mit dem Auto fahren. Was sagst du dazu?
We could go by car. What do you say?
A Das isch en guete Vorschlag.
Das ist ein guter Vorschlag.
That's a good suggestion.
B Häsch du en Stadtplan vo Züri?
Hast du einen Stadtplan von Zürich?
Do you have a map of Zürich?

Sources & further reading

This guide cross-references the following sources. The German Wikipedia article on Zürich German and Albert Weber's grammar are the most thorough starting points; Salzmann's papers are essential reading for the syntax of Zürich German relative clauses.

  1. de.wp Zürichdeutsch, Wikipedia (German)

    The most thorough freely-available overview. Phonology, morphology, regional sub-dialects, vocabulary.

  2. en.wp Zurich German, Wikipedia (English)

    Shorter English summary of the same material.

  3. als.wp Züritüütsch, Alemannic Wikipedia

    Native-Alemannic article, useful for confirming spelling conventions.

  4. Verein Verein Züritüütsch, Schreibweise

    The Verein Züritüütsch's official guide to spelling Zürich German, broadly following Dieth.

  5. Dieth Dieth-Schreibung, Wikipedia

    Eugen Dieth's 1938 spelling system for Swiss German, still the academic standard.

  6. Nthuleen Nthuleen, Swiss German Morphology and Lexicon

    Concise morphological tables (articles, pronouns) and lexical commentary.

  7. Salzmann Martin Salzmann (2006), Resumptive prolepsis in Zurich German relative clauses (PDF)

    The definitive linguistic treatment of wo-relatives and resumptive pronouns.

  8. tal-mi-or tal-mi-or.ch, Schweizerdeutsch Verben konjugieren

    Useful conjugation tables for Swiss German verbs.

  9. tal-mi-or tal-mi-or.ch, Grammatik Schweizerdeutsch

    General Swiss German grammar reference.

  10. Nübling Damaris Nübling, Die Kurzverben im Schweizerdeutschen (PDF)

    Academic paper on the "short verbs" (haa, gah, etc.) and their Konjunktiv II.

  11. Erb 2009 Maria Christine Erb, Die tun-Periphrase im Deutschen. Gebrauch und Funktion (2009)

    Survey of the tun-periphrasis across German varieties. Documents the construction's higher frequency in Alemannic dialects (Swiss German included) and its function as a Konjunktiv II auxiliary and durative/progressive marker.

  12. IPA Stephan Schmid, Illustrations of the IPA: Zurich German

    Reference IPA description of Zürich German phonology.

  13. eldrid eldrid.ch, Advanced Swiss German notes

    Practical learner-oriented notes by a long-term Swiss German student.

  14. Weber Albert Weber, Zürichdeutsche Grammatik. Ein Wegweiser zur guten Mundart. Zürich: Schweizer Spiegel, 1948 (rev. eds.).

    The classic descriptive grammar of Zürich German. Print only; cited via Wikipedia.
